FIRST PRINCIPLES

The world is assembled from cut paper, dry ink and printed matter — never digitally painted. Richness comes from composition, texture and handcrafted imperfection, not from detail.

1 · SILHOUETTE FIRST. Every gameplay object must read as a pure black shape. If you threshold the frame to two colors and can't tell spike from platform from player, the design has failed — no amount of texture rescues it.
2 · GAMEPLAY IS INK, DECOR IS PAPER. Everything you can touch is charcoal black (plus one restrained accent for its function). Everything you can't touch is tinted paper behind it. The eye learns this contract in seconds.
3 · FUNCTION GETS THE ACCENT. Color marks behavior, not beauty: terracotta = danger and goal, teal = logic and zones, pink = soft and breakable, lavender = exits and altered rules. An accent that doesn't mean something is noise.
4 · NEGATIVE SPACE IS A MATERIAL. Large open cream areas are deliberate composition, not emptiness waiting to be filled. Cutting a decor piece is usually the improvement.
5 · JITTER THE SHAPE, NEVER THE HITBOX. Colliders stay clean rectangles at exact grid positions; only the drawn polygon wobbles. Rough edges are a costume the physics never wears.
6 · IMPERFECT, NOT SLOPPY. Vertex jitter stays within ±2px, wear erases at most ~15% of a fill. Past that the object stops looking printed and starts looking broken.
7 · ABSENCE HAS A COSTUME TOO. Things that vanish, haven't happened yet, or only exist as rules (zones, fake platforms, collapsed floors) are drawn as dashed outlines. Dashes mean "the idea of a thing."
8 · SAME FUNCTION, SAME SHAPE, EVERYWHERE. A spike is always triangles, a plate is always a terracotta dome, an eye always means "this watches you." The vocabulary never gets synonyms.

COMPOSITION PATTERNS

Every screen should hold together like a magazine spread: one oversized anchor shape (a giant circle, a torn panel, a black paper block), one counterweight on the opposite third, and small clustered details (birds, crosses, scribbles) that guide the eye along the play route.

  • Fixed layer order, composed bottom-up: paper ground → soft tinted stains → oversized collage pieces → symbols, splatters and grain → tiles → static entities → dynamic entities → labels/UI. Nothing ever draws out of its layer.
  • Tiny library, infinite screens. Reuse the same ~16 decor assets everywhere; the hand-assembled feel comes from randomizing position, scale, rotation, opacity and tint per placement — never from authoring new art.
  • Decor obeys a no-fly zone. Nothing decorative within half a tile of a walkable surface or hazard, and decor contrast stays below gameplay contrast (tints and low alpha, never full ink).
  • One or two accents per level. Pick the level's accent pair up front and tint stains, torn paper and collage pieces from it. Harmony is a budget, and it's small.
  • Detail clusters in odd numbers, placed where you want the eye to travel next — usually just above the intended route.

  • Accents sit on ink or on cream — never on each other. Terracotta teeth on a black crusher works; terracotta on teal vibrates and cheapens both.
  • Backgrounds use accents at 10–20% alpha as stains, ~85% as cut paper. Full-strength accent is reserved for gameplay meaning.
  • Pure white exists only in the player's eyes and tiny highlights — everything else "white" is cream, so the page never breaks the paper illusion.

PROCEDURAL INK TRICKS

VERTEX JITTER: SUBDIVIDE EDGES ~9PX, WOBBLE ±1.5PX
DRY-BRUSH WEAR: NONE → LIGHT → HEAVY (destination-out)
BRUSH OVERSHOOT: STROKES PAST THE TOP EDGE SELL THE PRINT
SEEDED RNG: SAME ASSET, THREE SEEDS — NEVER TWINS, ALWAYS DETERMINISTIC
TWO-ARC HALF FILL: LAMPS, BLADES, MOONS FROM ONE MOVE
SPLATTER: DISTANCE² CLUSTERING READS AS FLICKED INK
  • Rough polygon: walk each edge in ~9px steps and offset every step by ±jitter. One helper gives every rectangle, triangle and slab in the game its cut-paper edge.
  • Wear: switch to destination-out and erase low-alpha specks plus a few thin scratches. Erasing reads as missing ink; painting light spots on top reads as dirt — always erase.
  • Grain: 1px specks, half dark half near-white, at 4–7% alpha. Cheap, and it unifies every surface into the same paper stock.
  • Splatter: offset droplets by rnd()·rnd()·radius — squaring the roll clusters them near the center like real flicked ink.
  • Seed from identity, not randomness: hash the entity id or grid position into the RNG. Every platform is unique, yet identical on every reload and every death.
  • States are frames, not filters: door open/closed, laser on/off, floor intact/cracking are separately cached bitmaps the engine swaps by key — never runtime tinting on one bitmap.
  • Hand-drawn strokes: the same jitter walk applied to a single line gives ropes, ladders, arrows, lashes and scribbles — one primitive, a dozen assets.

PERFORMANCE HABITS

GENERATE NEVER-PER-FRAME. Procedural drawing happens at three moments only: asset creation, level load, and appearance change. The frame loop is exclusively drawImage() of cached canvases plus the handful of kinetic entities.
CACHE BY KEY, PAD FOR JITTER. One Map keyed kind:state:size. Render sprites with ~6px padding so wobble and overshoot survive without clipping, and blit with that offset baked in.
MERGE BEFORE YOU DRAW. Collapse solid tile runs into one bitmap per run — 14 blits instead of 96 for this fixture, and the rough edge flows continuously instead of repeating per tile.
ONE BACKGROUND BITMAP PER LEVEL. Compose the entire collage once at full world size; the camera is a drawImage source-rect crop. Scrolling costs the same as standing still.
KEEP THE DYNAMIC LIST TINY. Split static/dynamic at load. In this fixture only the platform, hazard, laser phase, conveyor sweep and spring bob tick per frame — everything else is dead weight the loop never touches.
CAP THE PIXEL RATIO. DPR 2 for gameplay, 1.5 for big poster sheets. Past that you pay 4× the pixels for sharpness nobody perceives on textured art.
AVOID THE SLOW PATHS IN THE HOT LOOP. No shadowBlur, no filter, no save/restore churn, reset setLineDash([]) after every dashed pass, and keep blit coordinates integer-aligned where the art allows.

MEMORY HABITS

  • Budget before you cache: a canvas costs w·h·4·DPR² bytes. This fixture's whole library is a few MB; a careless "variant per instance" policy is how paper games balloon to hundreds.
  • Variants by seed, not by storage. Three or four cached variants per asset, chosen by position hash, beat fifty stored uniques — the eye can't tell past four.
  • Scope caches to the level. Background and merged tile runs die with the level; only the shared kind/state library survives across loads. Clear the Map on transition and let GC do its job.
  • Prefer one big bitmap over many small draws, but split anything wider than ~4096px into slices — mobile GPUs quietly downsample oversized canvases.
  • Never hold contexts you don't need: keep the canvas, drop the generator state. The draw functions are pure and can rebuild anything from a seed.

GOTCHAS FROM THE TRENCHES

  • Bound every procedural scan. This very page shipped a blank screen because a tile-run scan trusted tileAt(), which reports out-of-bounds as solid — and walked off the map forever. Loops that read generated data get explicit bounds, no exceptions.
  • Erasure needs its own layer. destination-out wear punches holes through everything beneath it — apply it inside the padded sprite canvas, never on the composed scene.
  • Draw text twice. Canvas labels render before webfonts load; paint immediately with the fallback, repaint once on document.fonts.ready.
  • Squint test every new entity: shrink it to 50% and desaturate. If it isn't instantly nameable, simplify the silhouette before adding any texture.
  • Randomize decor at compose time, never at draw time — a placement that shimmers between frames instantly betrays the handmade illusion.
